# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Intermediate-to-advanced skiers — typically mid-20s to mid-40s — who upgrade their kit regularly and want a straight answer about what their old gear is worth before they list it or trade it in. They've been on snow for years. They know their Salomon Shift from their Marker Kingpin. They don't need educating; they need a number. The mindset on landing: "I've been eyeing next season's skis. How much can I get for what I have?"

Secondary: buyers hunting value in the secondhand market. Present at launch but not the primary persona.

## Product Purpose

PowVal gives skiers and snowboarders an instant, realistic resale estimate for their used gear. No guesswork, no trawling eBay for comps, no awkward DMs asking "is this fair?" — just enter the gear and get a range grounded in real market data. A waitlist captures the early audience before the full marketplace (buy/sell) launches.

Success at this stage: credibility. If a skier lands on the page and thinks "yeah, these people know gear prices," the waitlist conversion follows.

## Brand Personality

Confident, Alpine, Direct

Voice tone: talks like a seasoned patroller, not a startup. Short sentences. No hedging. Price talk is practical, not exciting. The brand knows the hill; it doesn't need to perform knowing the hill.

## Anti-references

- **Facebook Marketplace / eBay** — no dense-classifieds energy, no crowded grid, no transaction-database aesthetics
- **evo.com / REI** — no outdoor-retail catalogue feel, no gear-photo grids, no "community" aspirational copy
- **Generic SaaS (Notion/Linear aesthetic)** — no restrained-cream-and-slate startup landing, no "seamless" copy
- **Ski resort marketing (Vail/Ikon)** — no glossy powder-day hero photography, no lifestyle-luxury positioning

## Design Principles

1. **Assume expertise** — don't explain what a ski is. The visual language and copy assume the user already knows; condescension is a conversion killer for this audience.
2. **The estimate is the product** — every page element should point toward entering your gear and getting a number. Form, CTA, and waitlist are the only things that matter; decorative sections earn their space only if they reinforce trust.
3. **Mountain-grounded, not resort-polished** — aesthetics should trace back to real on-mountain experience: the flat light of a February morning, the worn-in edges, the wax smell. Not the brochure.
4. **Directness as voice** — the brand says one thing at a time. No parallel copy blocks. No lists of features. Short, declarative, done.
5. **Seasonal energy** — ski culture runs on a rhythm. The design should feel alive during season and intentional in the off-season. Not evergreen-neutral.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. Prefers-reduced-motion already respected for snow animation (keep). Color contrast at ≥4.5:1 for body text, ≥3:1 for large text against all backgrounds. No motion-gated content.
